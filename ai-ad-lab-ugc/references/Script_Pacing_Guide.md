SCRIPT PACING GUIDE — AI UGC AD SCRIPTS
==========================================
How to pace AI UGC ad scripts for maximum retention.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
PRINCIPLE #0 — THE READ-IT-OUT-LOUD RULE (BEATS EVERY OTHER RULE IN THIS FILE)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Before you worry about pacing, scene count, B-roll frequency, or emotional arcs —
worry about whether every line sounds like speech.

**Read every line out loud (mentally) as you write it. If it doesn't sound like
something you'd actually SAY to a friend on camera, rewrite it until it does.**

This rule matters more than any pacing rule below. A perfectly paced script with
robotic lines will fail. A slightly looser script with natural human lines will win.
Natural delivery > perfect pacing. Every time.

The rest of this file is about rhythm, beats, and structure. None of it matters if
your lines sound written. See `Natural_Speech_Rules.md` for the full rewrite rubric.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

NOTE ON LENGTH:
Scripts are generated as sequences of short individual AI clips stitched together in post-production.

TOTAL VIDEO LENGTH — HARD LIMITS:
- MAXIMUM: 30 seconds. Scripts must never exceed this.
- OPTIMAL: 20 seconds. This is the sweet spot for AI UGC on Meta and TikTok.
- IDEAL RANGE: 15–25 seconds. Land here on every script.
- If a draft exceeds 25 seconds, cut scenes until it's within range. No exceptions.

Every scene has an estimated duration. Sum them before outputting — if the total exceeds 25 seconds, cut body scenes, trim dialogue, or both. The 30-second hard cap is non-negotiable.

PER SCENE — HARD CAP:
- No single scene may exceed 8 seconds. This applies to every scene: hook, body, and CTA.
- If dialogue takes more than 8 seconds to say naturally, split it into two scenes.
- Write lean. Say it like you'd say it to a friend — no padding.

Default target: tight, punchy, 15–25 seconds total. Cut anything that doesn't earn its place.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SCENE COUNT GUIDELINES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Short (punchy, hook-forward): 3–4 scenes — Hook + 1–2 body + CTA
Standard (most ads): 4–6 scenes — Hook + 2–4 body + CTA
Long-form (complex product, retargeting): 6–8 scenes — Hook + 4–6 body + CTA

Default to fewer scenes. Every extra scene needs to earn its place.
If a body scene can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.

By awareness stage:
- Unaware → 4–5 scenes max. Hook is everything.
- Problem Aware → 4–6 scenes. Pain validation needs space.
- Solution Aware → 4–6 scenes. Differentiation needs to be clear.
- Product Aware → 4–6 scenes. Proof and objection handling.
- Most Aware → 3–4 scenes. Offer + CTA. Short is better.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SCENE DURATION GUIDELINES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

HARD CAP: No scene may exceed 8 seconds. If dialogue is too long, split it.
TOTAL CAP: Sum of all scene durations must stay within 15–25 seconds. Hard max: 30 seconds.

Every scene in the output must include an estimated duration in seconds.

Scene #1 (Hook): 2–4 seconds MAX
This is NOT a scene — it's a scroll-stopper. Keep it ultra-tight.
If the hook takes more than 3 seconds to say, it's too long. Trim it.

Scene #2 (Retention Hook): 3–5 seconds
Must function as a SECOND hook. This is where most viewers drop off.
Should create curiosity, deepen pain, or promise payoff.
Do NOT start explaining the product here — it's too early.

Middle Scenes (Body): 4–8 seconds each (HARD CAP: 8 seconds)
Each scene should have ONE clear job:
- Explain the problem (1 scene)
- Introduce the product (1 scene)
- Show proof or demo via talking head + B-roll (1–2 scenes)
If a scene's dialogue takes more than 8 seconds to say naturally, SPLIT IT.

Final Scene (CTA): 3–5 seconds
Clear, simple, one action. Match the awareness stage (see CTA Library).

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
AI VIDEO CONSTRAINTS THAT AFFECT PACING
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Each scene generates as a separate AI clip. The workflow stitches clips together.
These constraints apply at the clip level — one clip per scene:

1. No hands interacting with products in close-up. Casual holding in medium/wide shot is fine.
2. No fast movement. Slow, controlled motion only. Energy comes from delivery and pacing, not speed.
3. No readable text inside clips. All captions, CTAs, and text overlays are added in post-production.
4. One character only. No multi-person scenes.
5. B-roll must come from the AI-reliable list in the layout template.
6. Avoid dramatic lighting changes or extreme angle changes between scenes — identity consistency depends on consistent conditions.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
B-ROLL PACING RULES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

When to use B-roll:
- After 2–3 consecutive talking-head scenes, insert a B-roll break
- When describing product features (show the product, don't just describe it)
- When the script needs a "visual proof" moment
- When pacing feels monotonous

When NOT to use B-roll:
- Scene #1 (Hook) — keep it simple, creator only
- Scene #2 (Retention) — usually better as direct talking-head
- CTA scene — creator must be talking directly to camera

B-roll clip duration: 3–6 seconds per clip
Under 3s feels jumpy. Over 6s loses attention without voice/action.

B-roll frequency:
- For a 4-scene script: 1 B-roll moment (usually Scene 3)
- For a 6-scene script: 1–2 B-roll moments (usually Scenes 3 and 5)
- For an 8-scene script: 2–3 B-roll moments

Always use only AI-reliable B-roll types. See UGC_Script_Layout_Premium_Template.md for the full list.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
EMOTIONAL PACING (THE ARC)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Every good AI UGC script has an emotional arc — it doesn't stay at one energy level.
Best DTC UGC scripts follow one of these patterns:

ARC 1: FRUSTRATION → DISCOVERY → RELIEF → CONFIDENCE
Best for: Problem-Agitate-Solution, Direct Response
Hook: Frustration (fast, relatable)
Body: Discovery (curiosity builds) → Relief (product solves it)
CTA: Confidence (calm, helpful)

ARC 2: SKEPTICISM → CURIOSITY → SURPRISE → CONVICTION
Best for: Objection-handling, Skeptic-to-believer, Reviews
Hook: Skepticism (doubtful, real)
Body: Curiosity (wait, maybe...) → Surprise (it actually works)
CTA: Conviction (direct, certain)

ARC 3: CALM → PAIN → URGENCY → HOPE → ACTION
Best for: PAS, Problem-aware ads
Hook: Calm (relatable situation)
Body: Pain (intensify) → Urgency (this can't continue) → Hope (solution appears)
CTA: Action (clear next step)

ARC 4: EXCITEMENT → PROOF → TRUST → URGENCY
Best for: Product Aware, Most Aware, Reviews
Hook: Excitement (can't wait to share)
Body: Proof (here's the evidence) → Trust (specific, believable)
CTA: Urgency (get it now)

ARC 5: INTIMACY → VULNERABILITY → CONNECTION → RECOMMENDATION
Best for: Founder stories, personal testimonials
Hook: Intimacy (lean-in, personal)
Body: Vulnerability (share the struggle) → Connection (you feel understood)
CTA: Recommendation (friend-to-friend)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RETENTION DANGER ZONES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

0–3 seconds: THE HOOK
95% of people decide to scroll or stay in the first 3 seconds.
Both verbal AND visual must grab attention simultaneously.
If the hook is weak, nothing else matters.

3–8 seconds: THE RETENTION GAP
This is where most ads die. Scene #2 must act as a SECOND HOOK.
Options: deepen pain, add curiosity, promise payoff, reframe a belief.
Do NOT start with "So I found this product..." — that's a skip trigger.

15–20 seconds: THE MIDPOINT SLUMP
Insert B-roll, a product beauty shot, or a surprising proof point here.
Keep the energy moving — don't let the script plateau.

Last 5 seconds: THE CTA ZONE
One action. Clear. Simple. Match the awareness stage. Don't blow it.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
COMMON PACING MISTAKES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Too much talking, not enough showing
Fix: Add product B-roll. Show the product in context. Break up talking-head monotony.

2. Product reveal too late
Fix: Show or name the product by Scene 3 at the latest.

3. All scenes same energy
Fix: Vary the emotional delivery. Contrast = retention.

4. Scenes too long
Fix: Max 8 seconds per scene for dialogue. If you can't say it in 8 seconds, split it.

5. No visual variety
Fix: Alternate between talking-head and B-roll. Only use AI-reliable B-roll types.

6. CTA comes out of nowhere
Fix: The last body scene should naturally set up the CTA.

7. Script reads like a blog post, press release, or landing page
This is the #1 killer of AI UGC ads. The AI voice will deliver EXACTLY what you wrote,
so written-sounding lines produce robotic-sounding reads that get scrolled in 0.5s.

Symptoms:
- Uncontracted forms ("I am", "you are", "do not")
- Marketing buzzwords (revolutionary, premium, game-changer, elevate, unlock, discover)
- Abstract benefit claims ("feel better", "improved results", "increased energy")
- Adjective stacks ("fast-absorbing, clinically-tested, premium formula")
- Every line the same length
- Frontloaded claims that sound like a press release

Fix:
- Contract everything possible (I'm, you're, it's, don't, can't, I've, you'll)
- Ban every marketing buzzword — rewrite the line from scratch if one appears
- Replace abstract claims with concrete specific ones
- One idea per line. Split adjective stacks into separate thoughts or cut them
- Vary line length — mix 3-word fragments with longer unspooling thoughts
- Add 1–2 mid-thought openers per script ("So —", "Honestly,", "Okay so —", "Look,")
- Read every line out loud mentally. If you wouldn't text it to a friend, rewrite it

See `Natural_Speech_Rules.md` for the full 12 rules and 30+ before/after examples.

8. Scripting fast movement or complex actions
Fix: AI video degrades with speed. Energy comes from delivery, expression, and edit pacing — not from in-clip movement.
